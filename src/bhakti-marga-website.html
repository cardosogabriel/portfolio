The Problem

Bhakti Marga, a spiritual organization, wanted to have their main website redesigned so that newcomers could better understand the mission, and the offerings, and find a way to connect in person. I was tasked with leading the design and coordinating the project.

As the lead designer, I guided the team through the design process, from gathering user requirements to publishing a website in production. To deliver this project I worked directly with content writers, photographers, another designer, and a web developer. Stakeholders were two directors of the organization and one spiritual leader.

The Design Process

Discovery: User Research

Since there was a website that was being used, I wanted to understand the needs, desires, and pain-points of the current users. To get feedback, I led a UX research, partnering with three members of the media team. We teamed up in doubles and interviewed eight users. I consolidated the interview notes, summarized the findings, and presented them to the team. This research unveiled requirements for the website, and material to model personas.

Definition: Personas

From the data gathered in the user research, I identified behavioral patterns and modeled two personas. As we only spoke to current users of the website, I created a third persona-hypothesis to represent users who access the website for the first time. As a result, we had two primary personas and a secondary persona.

Product Backlog

The results of interviews, combined with the meetings with stakeholders generated a list of needs for the website. I was responsible for maintaining the product backlog and validating the user stories with the stakeholders.

Ideation: card-sorting exercise

Given that the website had a lot of pieces of information to cover, I led a card-sorting exercise, with the participation of another designer and a director. From this exercise, we could map the basis of the information architecture.

Information Architecture

From the card-sorting exercise, I've proposed an information architecture for the website, considering the multiple pieces of content needed. I met with the content team and with stakeholders, and together we adjusted the IA map.

Persona-based scenarios

I've worked with persona-based scenarios for journeys that required more interactions, such as looking for a centre near me. I use scenarios as part of ideation before going into wireframes, since it allows more flexibility and creativity.

Wireframes

I've used wireframes for sections of the website that we needed to create from scratch, such as the Bhakti Marga Community, which comprehended a search feature and a map. As I had already a components library in place, other pages of the website were created directly on the CMS.

Visual Design

The organization already had a brand and a design language, which needed to be adapted to the web medium. Some of the characteristics of the language were the usage of a sans-serif font, titles in uppercase, opaque layers over images, and background gradients.

Having a rich archive of photographs, I explored the usage of images to contrast with the chunks of text. I worked with three types of containers, and different grid compositions to make the pages interesting.

The Development

The website was built in the Drupal CMS. I partnered with a developer, who did the backend and more complex features, whilst I did page building and front-end development. We set up a website version on staging and I created the pages using components, which could be populated directly by the content team. I took care that the website was rendering well for tablets and mobile phones, which represented about half of the access. After published, the website was ranking very well in terms of performance, accessibility, and SEO.

User Feedback

After the website was released and been used for about a month, we asked users for feedback. We got 48 respondents, and the majority considered that the website matched the organization's branding, that the navigation was clear, and that the design was good or very good.

Takeaways

  • As the organization is rich in content, it was challenging to define which content to present, being clear but not overwhelming the users with too much.
  • One director was very opinionated about how the website should be. I needed to defend my design ideas by relying on a solid process with steps validated by the team.
  • The initial user research was valuable to gather user requirements and better understand the audience.
  • The close work with the developer ensured that the website behaved and performed well in benchmark evaluations.
  • The final survey showed that people received the website well and could navigate through the content, which was our goal.
