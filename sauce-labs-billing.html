Sauce Labs
New Billing Model

  • User research
  • Wireframing
  • Content Definition
  • Prototyping
  • Usability Testing
  • Visual Design

The Problem

Sauce Labs, the world's largest continuous testing cloud of web and mobile applications, wanted to introduce a new credits-based billing model to their self-serve customers. They had the product goal of increasing the number of paying customers, and the business goal of increasing revenue. The Billing product team was tasked with delivering a new purchase flow.

As the lead product designer, I established the design goal of providing a frictionless purchase flow that converted higher than the previous one. To deliver this project I worked directly with a product manager, another product designer, a content writer, a UX Researcher, and several developers. Stakeholders were the Director of Customer Experience and the Chief Product Officer.

The Design Process

Discovery: User Research

The billing team wanted to validate if our users were interested in a credits-based model. To get feedback, I've designed a call to action on our product pricing page, which asked if they would like to try a credits-based plan. That led to a survey, which I created with the PM. We could observe that there was interest from users to try a credits-based model; that users primarily think of testing in terms of duration of execution; and that users need to be able to monitor their usage to not go over the limit. The observations brought confidence to the team and insights into essential features.

Competitive Analysis

I started with a discovery of how SaSS services are presenting credit-based plans and their purchase flows. I also analyzed how our competitors presented their plans and checkout, as well as some well-established SaSS products for developers. Findings: On Pricing pages you can see a pattern of highlighting one plan among three or four, a list of features, a more detailed breakdown of cost, and a FAQ.

Ideation: Team Workshop

The team got together online for a workshop to define the scope of an MVP. It was an intense collaboration between the product manager, myself, and engineers. I advocated for the user to make sure the experience would be great. After three days we had a clear picture of what were the user stories we would like to cover: allowing users to buy a new credits plan, offering auto top-up if credits went low, and monitoring the credits balance.

Scenarios and User Flows

I used persona-based scenarios to envision positive experiences of our users with the product, which opened up to different ideas. After that, I created user flows to make sure all interactions were accounted for.

Mid-Fidelity Prototypes

Having user flows, I started to ideate on mid-fidelity prototypes and partnered with another designer. The initial ideation was around three concepts. On the left, are the options of purchasing the new or old plan, and further information about each plan is on the next screen. In the middle is the pricing page that was in place, and when clicking on Add Plan, the old and new plans are presented. On the right, the new and old plans are side by side, highlighting which plan is better for whom, with a direct comparison of features. I presented the concepts in design critique sessions and moved forward with the concept at the right, which made it easier to make a decision straight away.

Feedback: the CTO vision

Having refined the concept, we presented it to the CTO to get buy-in from the business. He had a different perspective on how to present plans, preferring to show only the new credits-based plan to remove friction. The con is that we would not have data to know which plan users prefer side by side. I've proposed to run an A/B test, offering the old plan to half of users, and the new plan to the other half. In this way, we could measure the winner. We worked on several designs that presented only the new plan.

Rapid Iterative Testing and Evaluation (RITE)

We wanted to test variations of the new pricing page. We involved the UX Researcher and decided to do Rapid Iterative Testing and Evaluation. We recruited internal employees who met our personas. I‚Äôve created Figma interactive high-fidelity prototypes, and we run the tests over Zoom. After four tests we adjusted the designs and ran four more tests. Observations: Developers wanted to have all the crucial information initially exposed in the UI; Developers could estimate how much time their tests take, and calculate the plan they needed.

Purchase flow for the MVP

I've proposed an updated pricing page based on the observations gathered during the testing. I went with a layout that presented the benefits of the plan and allowed the users to choose the amount of credits at the top, followed by a breakdown of pricing below, and a simple FAQ, all of them visible from the beginning (left image). In addition, I've updated the checkout page (middle image) and we created a success page for after the purchase (right image), which together formed the total purchase flow. The designs were done using Figma auto-layout feature and using the available design system components.

Post Design: Implementation and Analytics

I consider that the design is not done until it is shipped. As UIs were implemented, I reviewed them and collaborated with the developers to make sure they were looking and behaving as designed. To speed up the process, I put my hand in the code and adjusted some UI details, and CSS, and sent merge requests.

I kept monitoring the performance of the new flow in analytics tools, observing the purchase funnel in Pendo, the purchases in Looker, and how the users were interacting with the UIs in MS Clarity. Those observations led to opportunities for improvement that were brought to the team.

Website Pricing Page

Having an updated pricing page for logged-in users inside the product, there was the need to update the pricing page on the main website. I partnered with the Director of Customer Experience and worked on a design that was an increment to the product pricing page, showcasing a credit calculator. The company website was being redesigned with a new design language, so I applied the new language to the page.

Takeaways

  • It was a high-complexity project with multiple stakeholders. Collaboration was the key to delivering it.
  • Prototyping and testing helped us understand the user experience and feel confident in which direction to go.
  • Pair design was initially challenging but elevated the quality of the delivered UIs.
  • Close work with engineers was effective as UIs looked very similar to the designs.
  • Even though the new plan was converting better (higher number of subscriptions), it was bringing less revenue. By the time I stopped working on the project, we were experimenting with ways to increase revenue.
